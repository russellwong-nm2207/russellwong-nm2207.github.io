Rising BTO prices. Should you be concerned?

16 March 2023

It has been an established norm for university students to apply for BTO flats during the course of their studies to maximise the grants available to them. However, with increasing prominence of discussions over the affordability of public housing, undergraduates are starting to sweat over the possibility of the prices being out of reach considering the lagging growth in income.

Click here to read more about HDB's statement.

This article seeks to explore the price trends of 4-room BTO flats in non-mature estates. It then aims to explain the reasons behind the inclines and declines in prices throughout the period of 2008 to 2021. Finally, it provides informative graphical illustrations of the price trends and income growth of graduates in comparison.

While the graph does not show a constant incline of the price trend, it is evident that the price in the current climate is significantly higher than in the past. It is also notable that a lot of other factors affect the housing market such as new developments within the estates and in the country, introduction of policies and the general economic situation.

The following are general explanations behind the incline / decline seen in the graph above:
YEAR CHANGE REASON
2008 - Low price point due to ongoing economic crisis.
2009 Increase Slow recovery from the economic crisis that happened in the last year but start of Sale of Balance Flats (SBF)
policy that drove purchases up slightly.
2010 Increase Full recovery from the economic crisis and the start of major population growth that drove up demand for housing.
2011 Decrease Introduction of Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty caused demand to drop.
2012 Increase Unveiling of Thomson-East Line drove the prices up of surrounding estates due to potential increase in accessibility and amenities.
2013 Decrease Economic slowdown and increase in supply of public and private housing.
2014 Increase Increase in development pricing due to increase confidence in over-subscription.
2015 Increase Change in price insignificant.
2016 Decrease Return of oversupply causing prices to fall.
2017 Increase Easing of cooling measures by the government by cutting the Seller's Stamp Duty, and the return of en-bloc fever.
2018 Increase Revision of Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty Rates and cooling measures implemented drove the prices up.
2019 Decrease Introduction of Enhanced CPF Housing Grant (EHG) led to increased consumer willingness to purchase but median brought down
due to limited BTO options in the non-mature estates market.
2020 Decrease The economic effects of the Covid pandemic hit the hardest causing people to put a hold on major life investments like property.
2021 Increase Slight recovery from the Covid pandemic but the difficulty of attaining building supplies led to the increase in costs of building materials,
and on top of inflation, drove the prices to an all-time high.

While the graph shows a constant incline of the annual income of Singaporeans, it does not reflect the whole truth. With raging inflation and the government's decision to gradually increase GST to 9%, the slight increase in income does not compensate enough to the additional expense brought upon us. On top of that, the rise in pricing trends just shows that our ability to afford BTOs will continue to decline since annual income growth is not uniform to the growth of public housing prices.

Public housing should never be used as a for-profit tool. While some are struggling to put a roof over their heads or have a property to call their own, those more fortunate are treating BTOs as pawns to lap those less well-off in terms of wealth. Without some form of government intervention, no matter how large of a supply, this divide will exist and the promise of BTOs remaining affordable will be nothing more than a lie...
